About the Loma Theater

Discover the story behind Socorro’s beloved theater.

Our Journey

The Beginning

The Loma Theater opened its doors in 1937, situated on Socorro’s bustling town plaza. However, tragedy struck in December 1956 when a fire broke out during a showing of "The Day the World Ended." Though the fire destroyed the theater, over 200 attendees escaped unharmed.

After the fire, the search for a new location led to the historic Price-Lowenstein Mercantile Building. Originally constructed in the early 1900s as a general store, it became a National Guard armory in 1921 before being remodeled into the Loma Theater in 1959.

Unique Features

The original Loma Theater was designed with families in mind. It seated over 600 people and featured special "crying" and "smoking" rooms equipped with drive-in speakers. Parents could tend to their children without missing a moment of the film, while smokers had their own dedicated viewing area.

In the 1970s, weekend matinees became a community favorite. Owners staged fun contests for children, including aisle races and talent shows, creating lifelong memories for young attendees.

Renovations and Closures

A major remodel in 1986 reduced the auditorium size, replaced bench seating, and removed the iconic stage and special viewing rooms. While these changes modernized the theater, its charm remained intact. Unfortunately, financial struggles led to its closure in 2008 and again in 2014.

During its 2009 reopening as "The New Loma Stadium Cinema," attempts to revitalize the theater with stadium seating fell short due to outdated 35mm projection equipment. The theater ultimately closed again in 2014 as the industry transitioned to fully digital formats.

A Modern Revival

In 2017, a partnership between New Mexico Tech and First State Bank sparked the theater’s revival. With a digital projector system and 3D capabilities, the Loma Theater reopened in 2018 to enthusiastic community support. Ownership transferred to On the Break Entertainments, LLC, in 2021, ushering in a new era of first-run films and family entertainment.
